# The ~2.5°C World: Where "Current Policies" Lands Earth's Climate by 2076

## The setup

**T-zero is mid-2026.** "Current policies continue" is genuinely ambiguous, so I pin it to the mainstream **dynamic** reading — the Climate Action Tracker "Policies & action" line and the IEA's Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS): today's laws *plus* the renewables/EV rollout and near-term fossil-fuel peak they already imply, but **no new ambition** beyond what's on the books. The literal "freeze the 2025 statute book" reading (IEA's Current Policies Scenario) runs ~0.3°C hotter and is flagged throughout as the upper edge.

The five numbers that matter most, from search this turn:

- **Current-policies endpoint ≈ 2.6°C by 2100** (CAT, Nov 2025) — and this number has **barely moved in four years**, though it fell from **3.6°C in 2015**. The frozen reading (IEA CPS) gives **~2.9°C**; STEPS gives **~2.5°C**.
- **Warming today: 1.44°C** observed in 2025 (WMO, one of the 3 warmest years); **1.37°C** human-induced with El Niño stripped out (Forster/IGCC 2025).
- **Emissions are still setting records: 38.1 Gt** of fossil CO₂ in 2025, **+1.1%** — plateauing, *not yet falling*.
- **The warming rate has risen**: forced rate **0.27°C/decade** (2016–2025), up from ~0.20 since the 1970s; the raw rate reads **0.35°C/decade** but is El Niño-inflated. Models expected **0.24**.
- **The first planetary tipping point is already crossed**: warm-water coral reefs, October 2025.

> **2023–2025 was the first three-year stretch to average above 1.5°C** — a consolidated **1.48°C ±0.13** (WMO). The 20-year mean that formally defines Paris crosses 1.5°C in the **early 2030s**.

## How it unfolds

The mainline is a **decelerating gradient**, not a cliff — warming keeps climbing but the *rate* eases in the back half as the fossil peak bites. Second-order effects chain as follows:

- **T+0 (2026)** — Start state: 1.44°C, record 38.1 Gt emissions, coral reefs in irreversible decline. The land carbon sink has just recovered from two El Niño-weakened years — but sits **~15% weaker** over the decade than a stable climate would allow. *Mechanism: sink saturation.*
- **T+4 (2030)** — Fossil CO₂ peaks and plateaus (IEA's central expectation) as solar + batteries take the majority of new generation on pure cost. *Mechanism: Wright's-law cost curves.* Single-year breaches of 1.5°C become routine; aerosol "unmasking" from cleaner shipping/Chinese air quality keeps nudging the rate up. *Mechanism: loss of sulfate cooling.*
- **T+7 (2033)** — The 20–30-year average crosses **1.5°C**. Paris's headline ceiling is functionally gone as a long-term average, though not as a legal fiction.
- **T+14–24 (2040–2050)** — Warming reaches ~1.8–2.0°C. **2.0°C is crossed around 2050** (CMIP6: likely 2040–2050, effectively unavoidable by 2060). Global crop yields run **~8% lower by 2050 regardless of the pathway**; heat extremes and wet-bulb days multiply. Sea level is up **~0.2 m** — already locked in by ocean thermal inertia, near-identical across every scenario. *Mechanism: committed warming from past emissions.*
- **T+34–44 (2060–2070)** — Warming ~2.2–2.4°C. The AMOC is measurably weaker; the southeastern Amazon shows drought-driven stress; sea level nears **~0.3 m** and economic drag runs a **few percent of GDP**, concentrated in low-latitude, low-income countries.
- **T+50 (2076)** — Central landing zone **~2.4°C above pre-industrial** (plausible range **2.2–2.7°C**), with **2.5°C** crossed right around this window — earlier if acceleration persists, later if it reverts. Sinks weaker, further sea-level rise committed for centuries.

## The branch points

- **Fork 1 — What "current policies" means, and whether warming accelerates.** The definitional swing (dynamic ~2.5°C vs. frozen ~2.9°C at 2100) is ~0.2–0.3°C by 2076. Layered on top: does the elevated forced rate (**0.27+**) persist as aerosols keep thinning, or revert toward models' **0.24** once the 2023–24 El Niño washes out? Persistence pulls 2.5°C into the **early 2060s**; reversion pushes it past 2076.
- **Fork 2 — Does a fast tipping element fire in the 2.0–2.5°C band?** Most tipping elements (Greenland, West Antarctica) are *commitment-by-2076, realization-over-centuries*. But three are fast enough to be *felt*: **AMOC** (onset), **Labrador Sea convection** (~10-yr transition, in ~⅓ of models), and **regional Amazon dieback**. Firing flips the outcome from a smooth curve to a step-change with a fat, partly-irreversible tail.
- **Fork 3 — Does the plateau become a decline, and do the sinks hold?** 2025 was still a record. If the peak slips to the 2030s or land/ocean sinks keep weakening, effective concentrations rise and 2076 shifts up *regardless of the policy label*.

## End states

| Outcome | Odds | What gets you there |
|---|---|---|
| **On-track gradient** — ~2.2–2.6°C by 2076, no major tipping *realized*; adaptation strained but coping | **~50%** | The rate reverts toward the modeled **0.24°C/decade**, the fossil peak lands ~2030, sinks roughly hold. This is the CAT/IEA/Hausfather consensus mainline. |
| **Runs hot** — ~2.6–3.0°C by 2076; **2.5°C crossed ~2060** | **~30%** | The forced **0.27°C/decade** persists or climbs, aerosol unmasking + weakening sinks compound, and/or real policy backsliding pushes emissions above the current-policy line. |
| **Step-change** — similar global mean, but an abrupt regional shift lands | **~15%** | A fast tipping element fires in the 2.0–2.5°C band — **AMOC onset**, **Labrador Sea collapse**, or SE-Amazon dieback — converting the gradient into a fat-tailed, partly-irreversible jolt. |
| **Bends down** — <2.2°C by 2076 | **~5%** | Cost curves + tipping-point alarm trigger action *beyond* current policy; solar/battery/EV deployment keeps beating projections as it has for a decade. |

**~50% (gradient):** Four independent estimates cluster tightly at 2100 — STEPS 2.5, CAT 2.6, Hausfather 2.7, frozen-CPS 2.9 — and models overwhelmingly produce a smooth curve. Interpolated to 2076 that's ~2.3–2.5°C. This is the modal physics-plus-politics path and gets half the mass.

**~30% (runs hot):** The forced rate is *already* above the model number, aerosol thinning is a one-way push, and sinks are measurably weaker and wobbling. CAT itself assigns a **~33% chance of ≥3.1°C by 2100**. The analogue is the 2015→2024 record run: reality kept landing at or above the warm edge of expectations.

**~15% (step-change):** AMOC 21st-century onset estimates span widely and are hotly contested (Ditlevsen's median 2057 vs. skeptics who see no robust signal); Labrador Sea collapse appears in ~⅓ of good models; Amazon triggering runs ~50% under SSP2-4.5. At least one *realized* fast onset by 2076 is plausible but not likely — the 1998–2012 "hiatus" over-reading is the cautionary analogue against certainty in either direction.

**~5% (bends down):** Requires effort beyond "current policies," so it's outside the scenario by definition — but clean-tech cost curves have repeatedly surprised to the downside, keeping it non-zero.

## Early signals

- **Fossil CO₂ actually peaks by ~2030.** The IEA expects a mid-2020s peak; if the 2027–2029 Global Carbon Budget still shows records, the "runs hot" branch is live.
- **The warming rate through ENSO-neutral years (2027–2031).** If the trend holds near 0.30°C/decade *without* an El Niño, acceleration is forced and real — not variability. The pivotal near-term test.
- **AMOC observing systems** (the 26°N RAPID array and 34°S salinity-transport indicator). A continued freshening/slowdown signal is the clearest step-change tell.
- **Land-sink resilience.** 2025's sink recovered post-El Niño; watch whether it re-weakens in the next warm phase — a wobble raises effective CO₂ independent of policy.
- **Policy backsliding vs. deployment.** US IRA credit repeal, coal-plant life extensions, and new coal subsidies push *above* the current-policy line; solar/battery/EV deployment beating forecasts pushes below. Track both quarterly.
- **Low-cloud / planetary albedo trend.** If the record-low albedo of 2023–24 proves a persistent positive feedback rather than a transient dip, it argues for higher sensitivity and the hot branch.

## What this run can't see

- **Cloud feedback is the pivotal unresolved physics.** Whether the recent albedo drop is a lasting feedback or noise won't be settled until ~2029–2031 — and it's the difference between the gradient and the hot branch.
- **Solar geoengineering (SRM) is an unmodeled wildcard.** A unilateral deployment (or a sudden termination) could snap the global-mean trajectory in either direction on a timescale this run treats as fixed.
- **Carbon-cycle surprises** — permafrost pulses, a sharper sink collapse, or Amazon carbon release — sit in fat tails that a mainline gradient understates by construction.
- **Political and technological discontinuities** — major war, an energy-demand shock from AI/electrification, cheap fusion or direct-air-capture at scale — could invalidate "current policies continue" as a premise long before 2076. Fifty years is four-plus political generations; the base rate for a stationary policy world over that span is essentially zero.
- **The rate debate rests on a ~15-year window** contaminated by a strong El Niño. The single most likely way this run is wrong is that the acceleration is being over- or under-read *right now*, exactly as the 1998–2012 hiatus was.
- **2076 is a waypoint, not an endpoint.** The most consequential decisions this scenario makes — ice-sheet and AMOC *commitments* — deliver their impacts mostly *after* 2076, so a "manageable" 2076 can mask irreversibility already locked in.
