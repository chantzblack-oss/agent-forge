How Old Is the World's Oldest Fire?

Nobody has actually dated it.

March 1828: a farmhand named Smart sees smoke on a ridge in the Hunter Valley that won't clear — not by afternoon, not the next day. He climbs up, comes back down, and reports a volcano. He's wrong. What's actually underneath is older, stranger, and far less certain than almost anything written about it since.

1828 – 1829

The volcano that wasn't

Smart told the station he'd found a volcano. Newspapers argued for months; nobody sent a geologist for nearly a year. When Reverend Charles Wilton finally climbed up in 1829 — almost by accident — he found sulphur vapour, stumps scorched from below, and no volcanic rock anywhere. His conclusion: coal, burning underground. He never dug to confirm it.

🌋 No volcanic rock 🪵 Stumps scorched from below 💨 Sulphur vapour from cracks

A presumption, not a diagnosis — and it happened to hold up anyway.

1918

Where “6,000 years” really comes from

Ninety years later, a pastoralist named William Abbott paced out the fire's dead, ash-grey scar: 6.5 kilometres, by his own count alone — never independently resurveyed since. He assumed the fire had always crept forward at the metre a year it seemed to manage then. One measurement. One assumption.

6,500 m ÷ 1 m/year = 6,500 years

Almost every popular retelling since has repeated this as settled fact. It's arithmetic, not a dating method.

THE OTHER HALF OF THE STORY

A floor, not an answer

Guillermo Rein studies smouldering combustion at Imperial College London and has visited the mountain himself. His view: 6,000 years is the least it could be, not a confirmed date — because a fire's speed through coal depends on seam thickness, oxygen, and ground cover, all of which vary across 6.5 kilometres.

Nobody's measured the size, either

The five-to-ten-metre hot zone everyone quotes — something between a shipping container and a small house, burning around 1,000°C — is inferred from what vents to the surface. Some accounts say 1,700°C. Nobody has drilled in to check.

Climb the marked track today — four kilometres over ground still too scorched for much to grow — and you reach a platform crusted in sulphur, gypsum and alum, with a rotten-egg whiff of hydrogen sulphide even in summer.

“Unresolved” is the honest word. Not “6,000, confirmed.” Not “probably 600,000.”

THE FUEL, NOT THE FLAME

6,000 years is a rounding error

The coal this ember is eating — the Greta Coal Measures — formed in the Early Permian, roughly 260 to 290 million years ago. (You'll sometimes see 235 million quoted; that figure looks garbled, and I can't confirm it either.) Even the fire's wildest guessed age barely registers against that.

Coal: ~260–290 million years Fire, at most: ~0.2% of that
BEFORE 1828

Already known, already used

“Discovered in 1828” means “noticed by a colonist.” Wonnarua women were grinding sulphur and alum crystallised at the mountain's vents into medicine long before Smart saw smoke — among the earliest documented uses of either mineral anywhere, feeding a trade network colonial dispossession later severed.

A warrior dies defending his people. The woman who loved him climbs the summit and won't leave. Her grief becomes too much for the sky-god Baiame to let run its course — so he turns her to stone rather than let it kill her. Her grief becomes the fire that still burns beneath where she stood.

Some retellings inflate “among the earliest documented” into “the first culture on Earth” — a claim that's close to unprovable either way.

NOT A ONE-OFF

The world has more than one

Brennender Berg, Germany

Ignited in 1668 — a mining accident, or by legend a shepherd's fire. Goethe detoured to see it in 1770. At “only” three and a half centuries old, it makes Wingen's claimed six millennia look almost implausible by comparison.

Jharia, India

Burning continuously since 1916. A 1970s survey counted roughly seventy fires at once — though they've merged, died out and reignited ever since, even as the ground above keeps sinking.

China, ongoing

On the order of 20 million tonnes of coal burn in uncontrolled fires yearly, by common estimates — with perhaps ten times that much more sealed off as unminable.

ONE LAST CONNECTION

Why did the number stick?

Here's a question with no graded answer. Why do you think “6,000 years, older than the pyramids” spread so fast and stuck so hard?

The essay's own framing — a pattern, not a proof

Picture the mountain itself: one small live ember trailing a long dead scar. Abbott struck the spark in 1918 — one grazier, one guess. Every retelling since is scar tissue: distance added by repetition, not by heat. The still-hot edge, where the number is actually contested, is Rein's objection — small, and persistently underweighted.

STILL BURNING

What's still unresolved

No one's funded a real date for this fire. No one's traced exactly how “among the earliest” became “the first culture on Earth.” No one's built a clean side-by-side of the world's long-burning fires — Wingen, Jharia, the Brennender Berg — by what's measured versus merely inferred.

Somewhere in the Hunter Valley right now, a two-hundred-year-old newspaper argument is still, technically, unresolved — and about a metre further along than when you started reading this.
