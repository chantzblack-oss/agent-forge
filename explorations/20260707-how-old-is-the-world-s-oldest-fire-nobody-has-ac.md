# How Old Is the World's Oldest Fire? Nobody Has Actually Dated It

In March 1828, a farmhand named Smart, working at Cressfield Station nine kilometres off, noticed smoke rising from a scrubby ridge in the Hunter Valley — smoke that wouldn't move on. Not the kind that clears by afternoon. It was still sitting there the next day, and the day after. Smart climbed up to look, came back down, and told the station he'd found a volcano.

Word spread fast in a colony hungry for news: within weeks the *Sydney Gazette* and *The Monitor* — it wouldn't rebrand itself the *Sydney Monitor* until later that year — were running dueling dispatches, both still readable today in Trove, the National Library of Australia's digitised newspaper archive, arguing over whether the colony had an active volcano on its hands.

Nobody rushed to settle it. The better part of a year passed before a clergyman-geologist, the Reverend Charles P.N. Wilton, got a proper look in 1829 — and even then almost by accident, since he was in the district on survey work for the Australian Agricultural Company and simply made the detour. What he found ruled out the volcano cleanly: sulphurous vapour hissing from cracks in the ground, tree stumps scorched from below rather than above, and none of the igneous rock a volcano would have left lying around. What was actually burning underneath, he could only presume: a coal seam. He never dug to confirm it — the presumption just happened to hold up. But it was a presumption, not a diagnosis, and that distinction turns out to be the whole shape of this story in miniature.

That fire is still going. It sits beneath what's now called Burning Mountain, near the town of Wingen in New South Wales, and if you read almost any article about it, you'll be told the same thing in nearly the same words: it has burned continuously for around 6,000 years — older than the pyramids — creeping through the coal at a steady one metre a year.

Here's what most of those articles skip past on the way to the pyramids comparison: nobody has published a peer-reviewed date for that fire. Not a carbon date, not a core sample, not a formal geological estimate of any kind. The 6,000-year figure comes from a grazier doing arithmetic on a map.

In 1918, a pastoralist named William Abbott published a slim book, *Mt. Wingen and the Wingen Coal Measures*. Abbott walked the length of the fire's dead, ashen scar — by his own pacing, 6.5 kilometres of burnt ground, a figure that as far as I can find has never been independently resurveyed and is quietly still doing all the work in every retelling, this one included — and did the only sum available to him: if the front creeps forward at roughly a metre a year now, and the trail behind it runs 6,500 metres, the fire must have started roughly 6,500 years ago. One measurement, one assumption — that the fire has always travelled at exactly this speed, across 6.5 kilometres of mountain that isn't uniform in any of the ways that would make that true. Virtually every popular account since — magazine features, tourism brochures, television segments — has repeated Abbott's arithmetic as settled fact. Almost none of them mention that it's arithmetic.

Someone has looked harder — though "finally," which is how that pushback usually gets framed, overstates how buried it's been. Guillermo Rein, a professor of fire science at Imperial College London who specialises in smouldering combustion — the slow, flameless burning that consumes things like peat and coal — has visited Burning Mountain and thought seriously about Abbott's number, and versions of his skepticism have already surfaced in outlets like ScienceAlert and IFLScience over the past several years. They just tend to run as a paragraph near the bottom, underneath the same 6,000-year, older-than-the-pyramids headline everyone leads with — a small, self-demonstrating example of how a first number keeps its lead position no matter what shows up later in the piece.

What Rein actually argues is narrower than "Abbott was wrong," too. His line, in interviews, is that 6,000 years is a floor, not an answer: something close to *it's not just that it's 6,000 years old — it's at least 6,000 years old; it could be hundreds of thousands.* His reasoning is straightforward: a fire's speed through a seam isn't constant. It depends on how thick the coal is at a given point, how much oxygen can reach it, how the ground above sits — and all three change across 6.5 kilometres of mountain, so today's one metre a year tells you very little about the average over six millennia, let alone sixty. But "could be hundreds of thousands" is Rein's informed opinion, built from site visits and from modelling how smouldering fires behave elsewhere — not a dataset, a method, or a number that's been through peer review, and neither he nor anyone else has published one. What actually exists is a century-old grazier's single assumption on one side and a working combustion scientist's unpublished hunch on the other. "Unresolved" is the honest word for the gap between them — not "6,000, confirmed," and not "probably 600,000" either.

Rein's other claim is the more unsettling one: nobody actually knows how big the fire is, either. "No one knows the size of the fire under Burning Mountain — you can only infer it," he's said — and that same quote, or close variants of it, is doing uncredited double duty as the source for most of the specific numbers that circulate about this place, which is worth remembering before treating any one of them as independently confirmed. The figure repeated everywhere is a five-to-ten-metre hot zone burning around 1,000°C, inferred from what vents to the surface rather than measured at the source; other write-ups of the same site, drawing on the same small pool of quotes, put the temperature as high as 1,700°C. Nobody has drilled into the active combustion zone to settle which.

Which points to something stranger than "underground inferno" regardless of which number you pick. Burning Mountain isn't a vast furnace sitting beneath the whole ridge. It's one small hot zone — on that five-to-ten-metre estimate, something between a shipping container and a small house — working its way through a seam of coal a couple of metres thick: a single lit ember running along a ribbon of fuel inside solid rock, the way a cigarette burns down its own length without the flame ever touching the ash around it. Everything behind it — all 6.5 kilometres of Abbott's scarred, mineral-crusted, plant-dead ground — is already spent. Stand on the mountain today and you're not standing over a furnace. You're walking along a very long, very old scar, trailing a small flame that has already moved on.

The coal that ember is eating, meanwhile, is almost incomprehensibly old by comparison, whatever the fire's own real age turns out to be. It's part of what geologists call the Greta Coal Measures — Early Permian coal, laid down somewhere in the range of 260 to 290 million years ago, depending which stratigraphic calibration you use. (You'll sometimes see "235 million years" quoted instead; that figure looks like an old or garbled number still circulating in tourism copy, and I haven't been able to independently confirm it either — which, in an essay about numbers nobody rechecks, is almost too fitting to leave out.) Even the conservative end of the real range puts this coal tens of millions of years before Pangaea began rifting apart into what would, much later and in stages, eventually become the Atlantic Ocean. Next to that fuel, the fire itself — 6,000 years, 60,000, 600,000, take your pick — is a rounding error.

The other thing the "discovered in 1828" framing erases entirely: Wonnarua (Wanaruah) people weren't just aware Burning Mountain existed before Smart saw his non-volcano. They worked it. A 2021 paper by Cara Cross, "Our Country, Our Healer," published by ANU Press, documents Wonnarua women grinding sulphur and alum crystallised at the mountain's vents into medicines with mortar and pestle. Cross argues this makes Wonnarua people among the earliest documented users of both minerals medicinally anywhere in the world — a claim some secondary coverage of her research has since inflated into "the first culture on Earth," ahead of the Egyptians. That stronger version is close to unprovable in either direction: Egypt, China and Rome all have their own millennia-deep, textually documented sulphur-and-alum medical traditions, and weighing archaeological and oral-tradition evidence against written pharmacopoeia isn't a race with a clean finish line. What's solidly documented is that Wonnarua use was early, deliberate, and fed a trade network that colonial dispossession later severed.

There's also a Wonnarua account of how the fire began — the version told on the Wonnarua Nation's own site, not the one that's drifted into travel-blog shorthand. A warrior dies defending his people from raiders. The woman who loved him climbs to the mountain's summit and will not leave, and her grief becomes too much for the sky-god Baiame to let run its course — he turns her to stone rather than let it kill her, and her grief becomes the fire that still burns beneath where she stood. Set that beside Wilton's clinical 1829 notes on sulphurous vapour and scorched stumps and you have two careful explanations of the same hole in the ground, arrived at by completely different methods, neither one lying.

Climb Burning Mountain today — a four-kilometre walk through ground still too scorched for much to grow — and you reach a platform crusted not in ash but in sulphur, gypsum and alum: minerals steamed out of the fire below and crystallised the moment they hit air, fissures nearby venting a rotten-egg reek of hydrogen sulphide even in the middle of an Australian summer, and the marked track still the only place you're meant to walk.

Wingen isn't even unique. Germany has its own Burning Mountain, the Brennender Berg in Saarland, ignited in 1668 — either a mining accident or, by local legend, a shepherd's fire that burned down through tree roots into a coal seam beneath. It was famous enough that Goethe made a special detour to see it in 1770. At "only" three and a half centuries old, the Brennender Berg works as a ruler: it makes Wingen's claimed six millennia look almost implausible by comparison, and it's a reminder that a burning mountain is a recurring type of geological event, not a one-off freak.

It's also still happening, at scale, right now. China burns through on the order of 20 million tonnes of coal reserves a year in uncontrolled fires, by commonly cited estimates, and fire puts perhaps ten times that much again out of reach entirely — fractured, destabilised or sealed off as unsafe to mine, a different kind of loss than combustion but a real one. Coal has been burning somewhere beneath Jharia, in eastern India, continuously since 1916; later surveys, around the country's 1970s mine nationalisation, counted roughly seventy discrete fires active at once, though "the same seventy" is the wrong way to picture it a century on — individual fires there have merged, died out and reignited the whole time, even as the ground above keeps measurably sinking regardless.

Here's a connection that's mine, not established fact, offered as a pattern rather than a proof: the physical shape of Burning Mountain — a small, live combustion zone trailing a long dead scar of undisturbed ash — looks a lot like the shape of how the "6,000 years" claim itself has spread. Abbott struck the spark in 1918: one grazier, one assumption, one round number, in a book almost nobody read. Everything since — every magazine feature, every tourism sign, every trivia list, this essay's own headline before you got this far into it — is the scar: distance covered by repetition, radiating from a single unverified point, each retelling adding length without adding heat. The live edge, the place where the number is actually still being contested, is Rein's small, hot, persistently under-weighted objection — not suppressed, not secret, just consistently subordinated to the older-sounding headline every time it runs. I can't back up the general version of this claim — that a plausible number gets copied at a rate with no relationship to whether it's still true, until the copying starts to look like evidence in its own right — with anything beyond this one, unusually literal, example: a mountain that will physically act it out for you, scar and ember both, if you're willing to walk the 6.5 kilometres. Or whatever the real number turns out to be.

## Where the pop version oversells it

- The "6,000 years, older than the pyramids" line is a 1918 grazier's back-of-envelope arithmetic — one pacing of the scar, one assumed constant speed — not a scientific date, and virtually every retelling repeats it as settled fact anyway.
- "Nobody's checked" overstates the silence: Guillermo Rein's "could be hundreds of thousands of years" view has already circulated in mainstream science outlets for years — it's subordinated to the 6,000-year hook, not hidden from it.
- The "discovered in 1828" framing writes Wonnarua people out of their own knowledge of the site, and the fire-origin story that circulates most widely online isn't quite the one the Wonnarua Nation itself tells.
- Precise-sounding numbers — the fire's temperature, its physical size, how much coal China loses to fire each year, how many fires burn under Jharia — mostly trace back to a single interview quote or a decades-old survey, not independent measurement.
