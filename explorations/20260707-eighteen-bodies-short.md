# Eighteen Bodies Short

In June 2019, in Uganda's Kasese District, near the border with Congo, a five-year-old boy died of Ebola, part of a family cluster that traced back across the border to the epidemic raging in Congo. Two other relatives were confirmed infected alongside him — a three-year-old brother and their grandmother, three cases in one household. Transmission stopped within about ten days. Most tellings say two of the three died, the boy and his grandmother; at least one account puts the toll at three. Uganda's formal end-of-outbreak declaration — the WHO's standard 42-day-clear benchmark — didn't arrive until roughly six weeks later, but the chain itself never left that household.

For the investors whose returns depended on that exact number, this was close to the worst possible outcome.

Uganda's toll sat inside a World Bank contract that only pays out once at least two affected countries have each logged more than 20 confirmed deaths. Congo, next door, cleared that bar by two orders of magnitude — the epidemic that had reached into that boy's own family, the Kivu Ebola epidemic in the Democratic Republic of Congo, went on to kill upward of 2,280 people (DRC's own health ministry later logged 2,299) and infect somewhere between 3,470 and 3,481, the second-deadliest Ebola outbreak ever recorded. None of that counted toward release, because the contract wasn't watching total regional suffering — it was watching one line on a map, whether Uganda's own count crossed 20, and it never did. Uganda came in at two dead, maybe three: somewhere around eighteen short. The $45 million earmarked for exactly this scenario never paid out. Investors kept their principal, in full.

That contract was part of the World Bank's 2017 "pandemic bonds" — the end product of an idea Jim Yong Kim, a physician who'd co-founded Partners In Health with Paul Farmer, first floated in Georgetown's Gaston Hall on January 27, 2015. West Africa's Ebola epidemic had already killed nearly 8,800 people by then and was still climbing; it would pass 11,000 before the year was out. Kim's speech was a pitch, not a launch: the instrument itself, the Pandemic Emergency Financing Facility, wasn't built until May 2016, and the bonds weren't priced and sold to investors until June 2017 — two and a half years later. But the pitch never changed in the meantime: sell bonds to investors, pay them rich interest, and if a pandemic strikes, redirect the principal to the countries fighting it. Insurance for humanity, financed by capital markets instead of taxpayers. It sounded, and still sounds, hard to argue with.

Here's the part that doesn't fit the "well-meant tool that broke" story as cleanly as it first appears: the narrowness wasn't just an accident of drafting. Some of it was close to structurally unavoidable. And, on one insider's account, some of it may have been more deliberate than that.

To sell a security like this to capital markets rather than fund it with a check, you need a payout event rare and precise enough for an actuary to price. So the trigger wasn't "a pandemic is happening." It was the death-toll floor already described, plus total case counts and confirmed spread across multiple countries, plus one further condition that outweighed all the others: at the exact moment someone checked, the outbreak had to be mathematically accelerating — positive exponential growth over a rolling window. That last clause could override everything before it. A country could clear every cumulative bar — cases, countries, bodies — and still not trigger a payout, if by the time anyone checked, containment had already bent the growth curve downward. The instrument wasn't just measuring suffering. It was measuring suffering that was still speeding up.

That much — the stack of conditions, the acceleration clause — is close to structurally mandatory for any parametric instrument sold on capital markets rather than funded by appropriation. It's a plain function of what actuaries can price, not evidence of anyone's intent. The sharper claim comes from Olga Jonas, the World Bank's own longtime pandemic economist, who told *Nature* in 2019: "The triggers had to be late and they had to be convoluted and complex to reduce the probability that the financing would be triggered." Plenty of people inside the Bank, she added, came to know it wouldn't work — though she didn't say, and *Nature* didn't clarify, whether that knowledge preceded the design or grew out of watching the design fail in practice. Read generously, the whole apparatus is a pricing mechanism that happened to disserve the people named as beneficiaries on the brochure. Read the way Jonas seems to intend it, it's closer to a mechanism built, by people who understood the tradeoff, to look like protection without often having to provide it. The reporting supports the first reading solidly. It supports the second only as far as one former insider's word carries — real evidential weight, given her position inside the institution, but a characterization, not a paper trail.

You can see why the job on the brochure needed help. When the bonds sold in 2017, demand ran 200% over supply from yield-starved institutional buyers — asset managers like Amundi and Baillie Gifford among them — at coupons of roughly 6.5 to 11.1 percentage points over LIBOR, territory shared with the riskiest junk corporate debt. In a near-zero-interest-rate world, a bond backed by pandemic risk read to a bond desk as exotic yield, not existential threat. That yield only pencils out if the payout is genuinely unlikely. The rarer the trigger, the better the trade.

COVID replayed the same mechanism at planetary scale. The World Health Organization declared a pandemic on March 11, 2020. The bonds didn't pay until April 17 — more than five weeks later, with global deaths already past 150,000 — because the contract required a minimum twelve-week window of sustained growth plus a data-verification lag before any money could move. Structurally, it could not have arrived any sooner. It was built to be a lagging indicator, and on that count it performed exactly as designed.

Even then, what arrived was smaller than the sticker price implied, and the sticker price itself only tells part of the story. The instrument paired each bond class with a parallel swap: $95 million in Class B bonds plus a $55 million swap, $225 million in Class A bonds plus a $50 million swap, $425 million in capital at risk altogether once both are counted. Class B was wiped out entirely; Class A lost 16.67% of its principal. The combined payout, $195.84 million, only reconciles once the swaps are counted alongside the bonds investors actually traded. And bondholders hadn't been sitting empty-handed waiting for a crisis — over the life of the bonds they'd already banked something like $96 million in coupon interest, according to financial-press tallies not independently checked here against a primary Bank filing. On that same accounting, Class B investors were on pace for roughly a 40% three-year return if COVID had never happened at all. The instrument sold as pandemic insurance had, for its holders, functioned as a good bet that paid off whether or not the bet ever came due.

The idea's own biggest champion eventually admitted as much. Larry Summers called pandemic bonds a "win-win-win" in 2015. In a private email in February 2020 — as COVID was already spreading — he called the World Bank's execution "a dumb idea," comparing it, per Bloomberg's later reporting (which Summers reportedly confirmed), to "insuring my toaster." He later called it, publicly, at a Center for Global Development event, "an embarrassing mistake" born of "financial goofiness" — accounts differ slightly on exactly when he said it, but all place it after COVID, not before. Same man, same idea, a public verdict delivered only once the bill came due.

Jim Yong Kim had already moved on. Four years after Georgetown, in January 2019, he abruptly resigned the World Bank presidency to join Global Infrastructure Partners, an infrastructure investment firm — not the same asset class as pandemic bonds, but the same general world of private institutional capital he'd spent four years courting to underwrite pandemic response. Medical anthropologist Susan Erikson's 2025 book *Investable!*, drawn from fieldwork spanning West African villages to trading floors in London and New York, argues the episode is better read not as a good tool gone wrong but as the first sustained experiment in turning epidemic mortality into an asset class — and asks who's accountable once death has a price attached.

That experiment now has something like a control group. In 2026, a different Ebola virus species, Bundibugyo virus, hit the same region — Congo and Uganda again. The WHO declared a global health emergency on May 17. Within about three weeks, the Pandemic Fund — a grant-based multi-donor trust established in 2022, about eighteen months after the bond program wound down, financing preparedness broadly rather than paying out on one parametric trigger — had authorized up to $220.6 million. No capital markets. No exponential-growth clause. No death count split across two named countries. Just money, reprogrammed and moved — faster, and off a simpler trigger, than the bonds ever allowed. It wasn't a clean success either: the Independent Panel for Pandemic Preparedness and Response was, in that same window, publicly warning that the money wasn't reaching the front line fast enough. Faster than an instrument that structurally couldn't move for five weeks is not a high bar. It isn't the same as fast enough.

Here's a way to see the whole affair that finance coverage rarely reaches for — and this connection is my own framing, not the World Bank's or Erikson's. Every early-warning system — smoke detector, cancer screening test, pandemic bond — sits on a dial between two kinds of failure. Set it sensitive and you catch real emergencies early, at the cost of constant false alarms. Set it strict and false alarms vanish, but so does your lead time on the fire that's real.

Medicine has argued about where that dial should sit on a mammogram for decades — through guideline panels, contested statistical literature, years of public dispute over how many false positives are worth one earlier cancer catch. It's a real fight, and an unresolved one, but it's a diffuse fight: no single accountable party owns the mammogram threshold, which is exactly what makes it a fair comparison and an unfair one at the same time. Pandemic bonds never had that diffusion. A small set of counterparties set the dial, and that set paid only for one kind of mistake. Capital markets bore the cost of a trigger that fired on an outbreak that fizzled. Kasese, and Beni, and eventually a locked-down planet, bore the cost of a trigger too slow to matter. No published account says exactly who sat in the room when that threshold got negotiated — but the number that came out the other end reads like something a bond desk would set, not an epidemiologist, which tells you, if not who was at the table, then whose incentives were.

Call it a hypothesis, not a verdict, because one instrument is a sample size of one: converting a duty of care into a security doesn't just attach a price to the risk, it hands the pen that draws the line to whoever is best organized to defend their own downside. Pandemic bonds are the clearest single case of that dynamic I've found. Whether it's the rule or the exception elsewhere is the more interesting question — and not one this bond, on its own, can answer.

## Where the pop version oversells it

- The "designed to fail" reading leans on a single interview — World Bank economist Olga Jonas telling *Nature* that Bank staff came to know the triggers wouldn't work. The more mundane explanation, that narrow triggers are close to structurally required to price a security for capital markets at all, is equally well supported by the same reporting, and the two aren't the same claim.
- "Replaced" flatters the 2022 Pandemic Fund's continuity with the bond program. There's an 18-month gap between the bonds maturing and the Fund's creation, filled by a separate interim mechanism, and a change in kind: a single parametric payout instrument became a broad multi-donor preparedness trust.
- The 2026 Ebola response reads as a clean "control group" in hindsight. In real time, the Independent Panel for Pandemic Preparedness and Response was publicly criticizing that same response for not reaching the front line fast enough — faster than the bonds is not the same story as fast enough.
- The smoke-detector/mammogram analogy is this essay's own frame, not the underlying reporting's, and it understates how contested and multi-party real medical-threshold-setting is, compared to the concentrated, few-counterparty threshold-setting that genuinely did happen in the bond's design.

## Open threads

1. Whether Olga Jonas's account of internal Bank knowledge can be corroborated — an internal memo, a second insider, anything that would settle whether the triggers' weakness was foreseen at design time in 2016–17 or only recognized once the bonds began failing to pay.
2. Whether the "whoever sets the dial pays only for false positives" pattern shows up in other financialized duty-of-care instruments — catastrophe bonds for hurricanes, sovereign disaster insurance, pandemic-adjacent parametric products — or whether pandemic bonds are closer to a one-off design failure than a template.
3. How the 2026 Bundibugyo response actually finishes: whether the Pandemic Fund's frontline disbursement caught up after the Independent Panel's early criticism, and what a full accounting of that outbreak looks like next to Kasese and the 2018–20 Kivu epidemic.
