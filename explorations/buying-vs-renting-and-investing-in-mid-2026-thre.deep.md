# Buying vs. Renting‑and‑Investing in Mid‑2026: Three Dials, Not One Answer

## The verdict

"Should you buy or rent in the US right now?" is a category error. The answer turns on three independent dials — your **holding period**, your metro's **price‑to‑rent ratio**, and whether you're the person who actually **invests the difference** — and at the extremes it's emphatic. Horizon under **~5 years**: renting‑and‑investing wins almost everywhere (**~85% confidence**). High price‑to‑rent coastal metro — San Jose (**PTR ~45**), San Francisco (**~36**), Seattle, Austin, Portland: renting wins even over a decade‑plus (**~80%**). Low price‑to‑rent metro — Cleveland and Pittsburgh (**~11–12**), Detroit (**~8**): buying beats renting *from month one* and breaks even in **under two years** (**~75%**).

The case people actually fight about — a median household buying a moderate home to hold **10+ years** — is the one where "buying is worse" is **not established**. The popular rent‑and‑invest math almost always compounds *pre‑tax* equity returns while ignoring the owner's tax‑free gains, forced‑savings behavior, and fixed‑payment inflation hedge — and most people never invest the gap anyway (US savings rate **~3–5%**). Mid‑2026's specifics — **6.4–6.7%** rates forecast flat through 2027 (no refinance rescue), flat‑to‑negative real prices now falling month‑over‑month, and an **8–11% round‑trip** the NAR settlement did *not* shrink — widen the renting edge at the margin but don't settle the long‑hold median case.

**Rent‑and‑invest is the better bet for short horizons, expensive metros, and disciplined investors; buying is competitive‑to‑favorable for long‑hold buyers in moderate markets. The 43:1 "homeowners are richer" stat is too selection‑contaminated to settle it either way.**

**Dial 1 — horizon.** Zillow's June 4, 2026 metro study — transaction‑cost‑inclusive, and it *already* assumes the renter invests the difference — puts the **national breakeven at ~6 years**, down from an **8.4‑year peak in October 2023**. Below **3–4 years renting wins almost everywhere**: too little time to amortize the round‑trip friction. The trap is that intended tenure badly overpredicts realized tenure — first‑time buyers *intend* ~15 years but realize **~4–8**, and the average owner has a **~1‑in‑4 chance of moving within 5 years**. A buyer at 6.5% is also *free to move* in a way the sub‑3% "locked‑in" cohort isn't, so don't assume today's record 11–12‑year median seller tenure for someone buying now.

**Dial 2 — price‑to‑rent.** The sign‑flipping variable (its own section below), and it sets how big the "difference" you'd invest even *is*.

**Dial 3 — do you actually invest, and at what return.** This is where the plan usually dies, on two legs that both must hold. The behavioral leg is brutal: disciplined monthly reinvestment of an ~$800 gap collides with a nation saving **~3% (May 2026)**. The return leg is genuinely contested:

| 10‑yr S&P forecast | Nominal | ≈ Real | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Goldman / Kostin (Oct 2024)** | **3%** | ~1% | 72% chance stocks trail bonds; 7th‑percentile outcome |
| **Goldman / Snider (Nov 2025)** | **~7%** | ~5% | Explicit walk‑back of the above |
| **Vanguard VCMM (2026)** | **4–5%** | ~2–3% | Mega‑cap tech valuations |
| **21‑manager consensus** | **~6%** | ~3–4% | — |
| **Historical, since 1926** | **~10.2%** | **~6.5–7%** | The "7% real" calculator default = the *optimistic* baseline |

> **Goldman's ten‑year equity forecast moved four percentage points — from 3% to ~7% — in thirteen months, with the market doing nothing dramatic in between.** That single fact is the strongest argument against betting a house‑sized decision on any point estimate of "invest the difference."

The framing you'll see everywhere — "7% real vs. Goldman's 3%" — is apples‑to‑oranges: the 7% is *real*, Goldman's 3% *nominal*. In real terms the honest spread is **~7% (bull) vs. ~1% (bear)** — and the invest side is taxed while the owner's side largely isn't.

Correcting both is honest — and neither helps buyers, because flat rates mean the "marry the house, date the rate" refinance escape hatch **isn't coming**.

## Where the sign flips: the price‑to‑rent map

There is no "US" answer because price‑to‑rent — home price ÷ annual rent — spans a **5–6× range**, Detroit ~8 to San Jose ~45, and that flips the sign. High PTR means a steep premium to own, so the capital left compounding in equities is large → rent wins. Low PTR means owning barely costs more than renting → little "difference" to invest, and you capture leverage and amortization → buy wins.

> **Renting is cheaper monthly in all 50 of the largest US metros for a starter home — yet buying breaks even in under two years in Cleveland and Pittsburgh.** Both are true at once, which is the whole problem with a national answer.

One live twist *strengthens* the low‑PTR buy case now. The stock objection — Rust Belt PTRs are low *because* those homes barely appreciate — inverted in 2026: **Pittsburgh +5.8%, Cleveland +5.9%** (top‑5 nationally) while the Sun Belt falls — **Cape Coral −9.6%, Memphis/Tucson/Palm Bay −3.8% to −6.1%**. Ken Johnson, co‑author of the FAU Buy‑vs‑Rent Index built for exactly this question, lands in the same place: **rent‑and‑reinvest** in stretched high‑appreciation metros, **buy‑and‑build‑equity** in Chicago, New York, and Cleveland "where prices are rising more moderately, leaving room for appreciation." The index designed to answer rent‑vs‑buy refuses to answer it nationally — which is the finding.

## Steelman: the case that buying is simply the worse move

The strongest version of the position opposite mine — that buying stays competitive for the median long‑hold buyer — is not weak.

**The friction is enormous and it didn't fall.** The round‑trip is **8–11%**, and the great hope — that the August 2024 NAR settlement would slash commissions — evaporated: Redfin shows buyer‑agent commissions **ticked back up to 2.42% by Q3 2025**, as buyers spent their new leverage forcing *sellers* to keep paying rather than negotiating their own fee down. You still lose ~a tenth of the home's value round‑trip.

**No rescue is coming.** Rates are forecast **flat at 6.2–6.5% through 2027** — no refinance escape. Prices **peaked in February 2026 and are falling month‑over‑month**, 2026 consensus **0–4% nominal**, i.e. **flat‑to‑negative real**. You're buying a **~5×‑leveraged** position in an asset whose long‑run real appreciation is **~0.5–1.5%/yr**, carried at 6.5%, while insurance (**+12%**) and property tax (**+27% since 2019**) climb underneath it — frequently a *negative net‑carry* bet, paying to hold an asset losing real value.

**And the disciplined investor genuinely wins.** Beracha & Johnson (1978–2009, 23 metros) is explicit: **if the renter reinvests the savings without fail, renting wins on average.** In high‑PTR metros the gap is a chasm — a decade compounding an $800–$5,000 monthly difference plus the un‑tied‑up down payment, at even a consensus **~6% nominal**, dwarfs the equity a flat‑real home builds. Ben Felix's "5% rule" (unrecoverable owning cost ≈ 5%/yr of value) actually *understates* owning today, since at 6.5% the cost‑of‑capital leg alone tops the 3% the rule assumes. On this reading, "buying is competitive" survives only by leaning on **forced‑savings paternalism** and a **selection‑biased** wealth stat. **~40% of me thinks this is the more correct read for the marginal person actually asking in mid‑2026** — someone in a pricey coastal metro, unsure they'll stay a decade, facing the worst friction‑and‑carry combination in a generation.

## The four things "rent and invest" quietly leaves out

That steelman has to assume away four real advantages — and for the median household you can't.

Here the channel's running trap resurfaces. In **"Who's Actually Paying for Your Cash Back,"** the error was treating a headline reward as free money while missing who quietly funds it. Rent‑vs‑buy runs the same trap both ways: *"renting is throwing money away"* ignores that owning throws its own money away (interest, tax, insurance, 8–11% friction) — **but so does the mirror claim** that "buying costs $920/mo more," which counts **mortgage principal — your own savings — as a cost.** Strip that out and the true *consumption*‑cost gap is far below 55%.

| Omission | What it's worth to the owner | The caveat that limits it |
|---|---|---|
| **Tax asymmetry** | Home gains **tax‑free to $250k/$500k**; imputed rent untaxed. The renter's equity returns are **taxed** (cap gains + dividends) | Exclusion **unindexed since 1997** — **13M+ owners** now exceed it and owe on the excess |
| **Forced savings (causal, not just selection)** | Turner–Luea (PSID): each year owned adds **~$13,700** net wealth *after* controlling for income, education, wealth, and prior savings habit | Neutralized *only* by a genuinely disciplined investor — empirically a small minority |
| **Fixed‑payment inflation hedge** | P&I frozen 30 yrs while rent compounds; a paid‑off retiree spends **~$520/mo** on core housing vs **$1,500+/mo** for an older renter | Taxes and insurance are **not** frozen and are rising fast |
| **Terminal value / long horizon** | Owner ends with a paid‑off asset; the renter pays forever | Realized only if you truly hold **20–30 yr** — realized tenure is often shorter |

The load‑bearing correction is the **43:1 wealth gap** — homeowner median net worth **~$430k** vs renter **~$10k**. It's contaminated by selection, leverage, and behavior; it does **not** prove buying beats a disciplined rent‑and‑invest plan.

> But "it's all selection bias" is *itself* overstated. Peer‑reviewed work that controls for income, education, initial wealth **and prior savings propensity** still finds each year of ownership causally adds real net worth. For the median household — the one saving ~3% and letting cash sit in checking — **forced savings is a genuine advantage, not a statistical artifact.**

Two the steelman skips. **Builder buydowns** on new construction — permanent rates into the **high‑4s%**, 2‑1 buydowns from **3.49%**, new builds often **$300–500/mo below comparable resale** — flip the monthly math in a real segment the "cheaper to rent everywhere" claim (a *resale* claim) misses. And **"rents are soft" is the wrong horizon**: today's flat rents are a supply artifact of record 2024 deliveries; starts are down ~40% and reacceleration is expected by 2027 — precisely the hold over which buying is supposed to win.

## What would change this answer

Triggers that would move the verdict, in rough order of impact:

- **A sustained mortgage‑rate drop below ~5%.** The break‑even rate where buying wins in high‑PTR metros sits near **4–5%**. Fall through it — a recession‑driven Fed cut, not the current base case — and coastal metros migrate from "rent" toward "neutral." *Single biggest swing factor.*
- **Your realized horizon lengthening past the local breakeven.** A firm 15‑year hold in a moderate metro flips the median case to "buy"; a forced move in year 3 flips *any* market to "rent." Discount your intended tenure for the ~25–33% forced‑move base rate before deciding.
- **Equity returns confirming the bear or bull case.** Sustained returns near Goldman's original **~3% nominal** (a 2000s‑style "lost decade" ran **−0.9%/yr**) gut the invest‑the‑difference side; continued **~10%** runs (H1 2026: +10%) fortify it. Given a 4‑point forecast swing in 13 months, don't make this load‑bearing.
- **Rent reacceleration arriving early.** If the 2024 supply wave clears and rents jump **>4%/yr** before late 2027, the fixed‑payment hedge strengthens the buy case sooner.
- **Congress indexing the capital‑gains exclusion** (before Treasury now). Re‑indexing restores a fading owner tax advantage — pro‑buy at the high‑appreciation end.
- **A price capitulation.** Prices are already falling MoM; a **10%+ national drop** resets entry PTRs and pulls breakevens in — improving the buy case at the new, lower price, painful as that is for today's owners.

**What would *not* move it:** foreclosure headlines (normalization, not distress) or any single bank's ten‑year forecast (too volatile to trust). If your evidence is a scary chart in a real‑estate blog, it's noise dressed as signal.
