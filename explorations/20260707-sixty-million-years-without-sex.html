Sixty million years, give or take

Sixty Million Years Without Sex

1687. Delft draper Antonie van Leeuwenhoek scrapes bone-dry dust from his roof gutter, adds rainwater, slides it under a hand-ground lens. Within the hour the "dead" grit uncurls into wheel-shaped animals, cilia spinning like tiny watermills.

He'd found bdelloid rotifers — and, without knowing it, the start of a 300-year mystery.

Enter the detective

A career spent telling threats from false alarms

By every rule evolutionary biology has for why sex exists, this lineage should have collapsed long ago. It hasn't. One investigator who'd spent a career separating real threats from false ones would eventually turn that same instinct on the rotifers — and become part of the story himself.

The bee-feces heresy

Yellow Rain

1983 — a heresy from bee droppings

Comparing alleged nerve-agent samples to bee droppings gathered around Cambridge, Massachusetts, Meselson stood up at an AAAS meeting and floated a heresy: the Reagan administration's flagship evidence of Soviet chemical warfare against Hmong villages — "Yellow Rain" — wasn't a nerve agent.

March 1984 — he goes to see for himself

In a jungle clearing at Khao Yai National Park, Meselson, bee biologist Thomas Seeley, and a Thai bee specialist watched the sky do exactly what villagers described — then got hit themselves, about a dozen spots apiece. A mass honeybee defecation flight. Pollen baked into the dried spots clinched it.

Why does radiation do nothing?

A 2023 study found this isn't a radiation-specific defense: it's the desiccation-repair system firing on damage it was never built to see. Lineages that gave up drying out for permanently wet habitats lost the tolerance right along with it.

Twenty-five years, four verdicts

The case: opened, closed, reopened

2000Case closed

Meselson & Mark Welch, in Science: bdelloid gene pairs are oddly divergent — read as eons without sex. Textbook "ancient asexual scandal."

2013Reopened

A Nature genome of Adineta vaga shows bdelloids are ancient hybrids. Those "divergent pairs"? Leftovers of one hybridization event — not proof of zero sex since.

2016→18Claim, then rebuttal

2016: direct evidence individual rotifers swap DNA. 2018: a rival team argues the samples were cross-contaminated, not exchanging. Disputed, still unsettled.

2020–22A statistical fingerprint

Whole wild-population genomes show linkage decay and allele ratios matching rare, unseen sex. Nobody has caught it directly.

Current status: probably not never.

Reality

Different toolkit. Tardigrades vitrify with the sugar trehalose; bdelloids make almost none. They lean on LEA proteins — borrowed from the family plants use for seed dormancy — plus antioxidants and chaperones.

"They evolved a shield against radiation."

Reality

No radiation-specific defense exists. It's the desiccation-repair system misfiring on damage it never evolved to see — species that stopped drying out lost the "shield" entirely.

The gene thieves' toolkit

About 8% of the genome is stolen goods

Borrowed from bacteria, fungi, and plants — among the highest horizontal-transfer rates known in any animal. Tap a heist:

🧬 A silencing switch, 2022

A bacterial methyltransferase gene, captured roughly 60 million years ago, now writes a DNA mark found in no other animal genome — used to silence jumping genes.

💊 Pirated antibiotics, 2024

Attacked by the fungus Rotiferophthora globospora, bdelloids switch on stolen bacterial gene clusters resembling real antibiotic pathways — upregulated nearly tenfold more in a resistant species than a susceptible one.

🧊 24 years in a freezer, 2025

Antarctic bdelloids, frozen at –25°C for 24 years, revived within an hour of thawing. Their freeze tolerance traces to a horizontally acquired bacterial ice-binding protein, confirmed by AlphaFold prediction and a lab-grown recombinant version.

❄️ 24,000 years in permafrost

A wild bdelloid pulled from Siberian permafrost in 2021, radiocarbon-dated to roughly 24,000 years old, also thawed and cloned itself. Freezing and drying turn out to be two separately pirated tricks, not one.

What's still open

Three loose threads

Catch cryptic sex in the act. Live imaging or multi-generation single-animal sequencing could settle the exchange-versus-contamination standoff directly, instead of by statistics alone.

Test the RAID hypothesis. If repair really leans on homeologous copies, foreign DNA should cluster where homeolog divergence is highest. Nobody has checked.

Mine the pirated chemistry. Do the stolen antibiotic-like gene clusters make a compound that works outside the animal? Open question.

Mid-repair, it accepts patches from strangers

Before we frame it for you — how would you describe a genome that does that?

Framing, not fact

The essay's own image: a genome acting like a RAID array, its ancient hybrid copies standing in as backup — except this array also lets a stranger's matching DNA patch overwrite the original. No paper has shown repair enzymes actually do this.

Meselson spent a Cold War career asking whether a signal was a real attack or contamination in the sample. He ended it studying an animal that never learned to draw that line — filing away foreign scraps for tens of millions of years, asking questions later.
