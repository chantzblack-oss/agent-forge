The cement brigade

Divers converged on Puerto Morelos, mixing cement on the boat decks "like contractors repairing a sidewalk." They hand-set roughly 1,200 coral colonies and gathered some 9,000 loose fragments before sun and currents could finish them.

The part the headlines skip

There's no sensor on the reef

The trigger is a modeled wind field — satellite, radar and ground stations reconciled into one storm map, arbitrated by a body like the U.S. National Hurricane Center. And it's no simple on/off switch.

Running since 2018 — 2019, by at least one careful account that dates the first underwritten policy a year after the trust itself.

The hospital and the ambulance

Why days matter

Most storm-snapped fragments never found new colonies — they die. And after Hurricane Earl (2010), broken fragments off St. Thomas grew white-plague-like lesions where they touched sediment and each other. Cementing fast outruns an infection clock.

200+ trained Reef Guardians by late 2022
"Like with medical insurance, you need the hospital and the ambulance in order for it to work."— Fernando Secaira, The Nature Conservancy
The uncomfortable question

Does cementing save the reef?

Triage that buys time

Lorenzo Alvarez-Filip has charted a 40-year flattening of Caribbean reefs. Gluing a branch back keeps it alive — but rebuilds none of the 3-D complexity that took centuries to grow.

A costly distraction

David Bellwood calls large-scale restoration "costly, premature, and doomed to fail" without emissions cuts first.

One fault line, two poles who've never replied to each other: buy time now, or stop pretending anything but decarbonization works.

Beyond Mexico

The model escapes the reef

2024 — a policy over Fiji's Lau Islands pays up to $450,000 for coral repair and emergency food and water. 2025 — a Maldives study prototypes a trigger on sea-surface temperature: bleaching, not a single storm.

But heat isn't a hurricane.

One last question

The essay's framing — a lens it offers, not a proven fact

Parametric insurance is a Geiger counter: built for events, a click at the instant something strikes. Wind is exactly that kind of quantity. But coral bleaching is a dose — a film badge that darkens slowly, read too late. Scientists already count heat in "degree heating weeks," a cumulative unit, because that's how the biology fails: not a snap, a slow poisoning.

The reef got a spectacular event-detector. It structurally can't carry a dosimeter for the slope killing reefs worldwide.

You can watch eighty divers with cement buckets show up for the spike — and count, precisely, the zero who show up for the slope.

The author flags this may not generalize to credit, flood, or disease models. Take it as a hypothesis the reef suggests, not one it proves.
