# The Reparations She Won

In 1870, a Black washerwoman in Cincinnati sued a white man for $20,000 — for the crime of stealing her life. Her name was Henrietta Wood. She was poor, had no birth certificate, and the man she sued was rich and politically connected. She won.

Wood was born enslaved in Kentucky around 1820. In 1848 her owner brought her to Cincinnati — free soil — and freed her. She had papers, a life, work as a domestic servant.

Then in 1853 a man named Zebulon Ward arranged to have her lured across the Ohio River, seized, and sold back into slavery. Her free papers meant nothing on the Kentucky side. She was shipped south to Texas, where slavery outlasted the Emancipation Proclamation by force. She spent roughly sixteen years re-enslaved.

When Wood finally got free after the war and returned to Cincinnati, she did not disappear — which is what the system counted on. She remembered Zebulon Ward's name. In 1870 she sued him for the years and wages he had stolen.

The case ground on for eight years. In 1878 a jury sided with her and awarded $2,500 — paid in cash by the man who trafficked her. Historians consider it the largest sum ever awarded by a US court in restitution for slavery.

And she used it. Her son, Arthur H. Simms, born while she was enslaved, was educated with that money and became a lawyer in Chicago. One generation: from a woman with no birth certificate suing for her stolen life, to her son arguing cases in court.

Wood's case is the most concrete answer to the abstract argument about reparations — because it already happened, in a courtroom, with a number, and it worked. The story was largely lost until historian Caleb McDaniel reconstructed it in Sweet Taste of Liberty, which won the 2020 Pulitzer.
