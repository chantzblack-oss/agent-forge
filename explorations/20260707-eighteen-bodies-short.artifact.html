A True Story

Eighteen Bodies Short

June 2019, Kasese District, Uganda. A five-year-old boy dies of Ebola. His three-year-old brother and grandmother are infected too — three cases, one household. Ten days later, the chain stops cold. A $45 million contract was watching. It needed far more than this to pay a dollar.

Kasese, Uganda — June 2019

The Instrument

In 2017 the World Bank sold investors bonds paying rich interest — 6.5 to 11.1 points over LIBOR, junk-bond territory. If a pandemic struck, principal would redirect to the countries fighting it. Insurance for humanity, underwritten by capital markets instead of taxpayers. Demand ran 200% over supply.

A bond desk doesn’t buy “insurance for humanity.” It buys a rare, priceable trigger with a fat coupon attached. Both descriptions are true. Only one was on the brochure.

Myth vs. Reality

Tap each card to flip it.

The claim

“It’s pandemic insurance — it pays out when a pandemic hits.”

The reality

COVID: WHO declared a pandemic March 11, 2020. The bonds paid April 17 — five-plus weeks later, after 150,000+ deaths — because the contract required 12 straight weeks of accelerating growth first.

The claim

Congo’s outbreak was the second-deadliest Ebola epidemic ever. Surely that triggered a payout.

The reality

Congo cleared the threshold a hundredfold. But payout needed two countries past 20 deaths. Uganda, where the chain reached, logged two. Eighteen short. No payout.

The claim

When COVID hit, bondholders lost their bet.

The reality

They’d already banked roughly $96 million in coupon interest. Absent COVID, Class B was on pace for a 40% three-year return. The “insurance” paid its holders either way.

Why So Narrow?

To sell risk to capital markets, you need a payout rare enough to price. The trigger wasn’t “a pandemic is happening” — it was death tolls, case counts, spread across countries, plus one clause that could override them all: accelerating growth, measured the moment anyone checked.

“The triggers had to be late and they had to be convoluted and complex to reduce the probability that the financing would be triggered.”— Olga Jonas, World Bank pandemic economist, to Nature, 2019

One insider’s account — real weight, not a paper trail. The more mundane reading, that narrow triggers are simply a pricing necessity, is equally well supported by the same reporting.

When It Actually Happened

WHO declared a pandemic March 11, 2020. The bonds paid out April 17 — five-plus weeks later, with global deaths already past 150,000. A twelve-week growth window plus a verification lag meant it structurally could not arrive sooner.

Jim Yong Kim, the idea’s original champion, resigned the Bank presidency in January 2019 for a private infrastructure investment firm. Anthropologist Susan Erikson’s 2025 book Investable! calls the episode the first real experiment in pricing epidemic mortality itself.

A Control Group, Sort Of

In 2026, a different Ebola species — Bundibugyo virus — hit Congo and Uganda again. Within three weeks of WHO’s May 17 emergency declaration, the Pandemic Fund authorized up to $220.6 million — a grant trust created in 2022, not the old bond program.

Bonds → COVID payout5+ weeks
Pandemic Fund → 2026 authorization~3 weeks

Faster isn’t the same as fast enough: the Independent Panel for Pandemic Preparedness and Response was, in that same window, warning the money wasn’t reaching the front line.

Who Gets To Set The Dial?

Every early-warning system — smoke detector, mammogram, pandemic bond — sits on a dial between two failures: too sensitive drowns you in false alarms; too strict costs you the lead time on the real emergency. Before we frame it, pick a stance:

The essay’s framing — a lens, not a fact

Medicine’s mammogram fight is diffuse; no single party owns that threshold. Pandemic bonds never had that diffusion — a small set of counterparties set the dial, and paid only for one kind of mistake: a trigger that fired on nothing. Kasese, and a locked-down planet, paid for the other kind.

One case. A hypothesis, not a verdict.
