# Who's Actually Paying for Your Cash Back

Picture two people back to back in a Target checkout line. One pays cash. The other taps a credit card that costs $795 a year to carry — a Chase Sapphire Reserve — and banks points toward a future flight. Both receipts show the identical price. Somebody is paying for those points. It isn't coming out of Chase's margins, and it isn't coming out of Target's.

Here's the plumbing that makes that true — with one honest caveat before we start. Nothing below is a traceable dollar moving from one wallet into the other's frequent-flyer account at that specific register. It's a store-wide, time-averaged accounting effect, blended across every debit tap, cash bill, and low-tier card swipe the store handles all year. The two people in line are a fair dramatization of that average, not a transaction-by-transaction pipe between them. Keep that distinction in mind and the rest of this holds up.

Every time a card gets swiped, the merchant's bank pays a fee to the bank that issued the card — the interchange fee. Merchants don't negotiate this fee individually; Visa, Mastercard, and Amex each publish sprawling schedules — well over a hundred category-specific rates apiece, sliced by merchant type, transaction channel, and card product — and as a rule, the rate climbs with how generous the card's rewards are. A regulated debit card sits at the floor: the Durbin Amendment — part of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act, though compliance didn't actually phase in until 2011–2012 — caps banks over $10 billion in assets at 21 cents plus 0.05% of the sale, about 22 to 24 cents on average, almost flat no matter the purchase size. From there it's less a clean staircase than a wide, lumpy spread: plain no-rewards credit cards tend to run merchants somewhere around 1.5% of the sale, and premium-tier products — Visa Signature, Visa Infinite, Mastercard World Elite, the tier a metal Sapphire Reserve or Amex Platinum sits in — can run north of 3%, with the exact number depending on which of those hundred-plus category rates applies. ("Metal" is just the card's physical construction; the variable that's actually priced is the product tier, which merely tends to travel with it.) Interchange tiers track rewards richness closely enough that most industry analysts treat the two as linked by design — networks pricing card tiers, in this reading, to hand issuers the pool of money they need to cover rewards costs — though that's an inference from the pattern, not a network memo anyone's produced.

In 2024, U.S. card issuers paid out roughly $47.5 billion in rewards: $16.6 billion in cash back, $6.6 billion in miles, and $24.3 billion in points and other benefits combined, according to the CFPB. That's real money, but it's a slice, not the whole pie: merchants paid something like $187 billion that year in total card-processing costs, a pool that also covers network assessments, fraud losses, issuers' cost of funds, and plain profit — and on premium cards specifically, issuers also fund rewards directly out of the annual fee itself and out of interest income from any balance a cardholder carries. Interchange is the largest single channel funding rewards. It is not the only one.

So a store pays wildly different rates depending purely on what's in a customer's wallet — a flat quarter on a debit tap, north of 3% on a premium card. The obvious fix would be to charge accordingly: cheaper prices for cash and debit, a surcharge on the metal card. For decades merchants simply weren't allowed to — card network rules, backed by a patchwork of state laws, banned surcharging outright. A 2012 settlement between merchants and the networks, finalized in 2013, began loosening those network rules, though "began" is doing real work in that sentence: an appeals court vacated the settlement's injunctive-relief provisions in 2016 for failing to adequately represent the merchants bound by them, and the underlying case has continued in one form or another ever since (more on that below). What survived is surcharging that's technically legal in most states today — by current counts, all but three holdouts, with Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Maine still enforcing outright bans (a 2025 attempt to repeal Maine's failed). It still hasn't caught on much. Part of that is plausible, if not rigorously measured, checkout psychology: a line-item surcharge is friction customers notice and resent in a way they never notice a few cents folded into the sticker price, and no single retailer wants to be the one store on the block charging extra. I don't have hard adoption-rate data to say precisely how "not much" is not much — but surcharging remains very much the exception, not the rule. So the average cost of accepting cards — cash customers folded into that average even though they generate none of the cost — gets baked into the shelf price instead. Everyone pays $4.99 for the cereal; the store just needed an extra few cents per dollar from card users to break even, and since it can't collect that selectively at the register, it collects it from everyone, cash included.

That's the transfer, and economists have tried to measure it — though "measure" oversells what's actually out there. What exists is a small number of modeled estimates that disagree with each other more than the popular version of this argument usually admits. A 2010 Federal Reserve Bank of Boston paper — its own title is "Theory and Calibrations," a label worth taking at face value — built a stylized model on late-2000s household spending survey data and produced a headline number: the average cash-paying household hands over $149 a year, net, to card-using households through exactly this channel. Because owning rewards cards and racking up heavy card spending both require good credit and disposable income, the modeled transfer ran uphill: households earning under $20,000 lost about $21 a year net, while households earning over $150,000 gained about $750 a year net.

A 2013 reexamination by the Payments and E-Commerce Research Council pushed back hard, arguing the original study overstated how expensive cash and checks are for merchants to handle. Change those assumptions, and the picture doesn't just shrink — in places it comes apart entirely. Under some of PERC's alternative inputs the cross-subsidy survives but shrinks to a few percent of merchants' processing costs; under others — including one of its own "conservative" scenarios, where merchants accepting cards is assumed to lift their sales by just 1% — the transfer's direction reverses outright, with card-using households subsidizing cash users instead of the reverse. PERC's own summary describes its range of results as "small, non-existent, or reversed" and explicitly declines to endorse either paper's numbers for policy use. So the honest state of the literature isn't "$149 a year, confirmed" — it's two modeling exercises that don't agree on the sign of the effect, let alone its size. What isn't seriously disputed is the channel sitting next to it: lower-income households that do carry cards tend to carry balances, and the interest and fees on those balances dwarf whatever is or isn't happening with interchange. And whichever cross-subsidy number you trust, if either, premium annual fees have climbed sharply since both papers were written: the Sapphire Reserve's fee for new applicants has gone from $450 at its 2016 launch, through roughly $550 in the years after, to $795 now, and Amex Platinum has followed a similar arc to $895 — though both increases hit new applicants first, with existing cardholders migrating only at their next renewal, so not everyone holding either card today is actually paying the current number yet. The trend points the unresolved number back up, not down, whatever it actually is.

A separate, newer 2023 Federal Reserve Board paper looked inside the card-owning population itself and found a related leak. It finds a redistribution that correlates strongly with zip-code income, education, and race — but attributes the core mechanism to a sophistication-and-attention gap among borrowers that the paper explicitly says cuts across income levels, not simply to issuers pricing low rates at poorer customers. (Some secondary reporting on rewards-card pricing complicates the picture further: interest rates on rewards cards can run higher, not lower, for low-FICO borrowers, muddying any clean "low rate as bait for the naive" story.) Whatever the precise mechanism, the distributional result is stark: the paper's estimate is that $15 billion a year flows from less-educated, lower-income, higher-minority-population zip codes to wealthier, more-educated ones, as disciplined full-balance-payers collect rewards subsidized in part by the interest and fees that revolving, less-attentive borrowers pay. Underneath both studies sits a group with no stake in either direction: the 4.2% of U.S. households — 5.6 million of them, per the FDIC's 2023 survey — with no bank account at all, who pay cash by default and help fund a rewards economy they're structurally locked out of.

Where this goes next is being fought over right now, in opposite directions on two continents.

In the U.S., merchants have been suing Visa and Mastercard over this exact arrangement since 2005 — the same long-running case whose first settlement got picked apart on appeal in 2016. A subsequent settlement fared no better at first: a federal judge threw it out in 2024 as too weak. A revised deal, announced in November 2025, won preliminary court approval in June 2026, with final approval expected later this year or in 2027. For the first time, it would let merchants flatly decline specific expensive premium cards, or surcharge and discount by card tier — attacking the cross-subsidy transaction by transaction instead of eating it silently. Merchant groups have a long, consistent history of calling settlements like this one inadequate; expect the same pattern this time, though I don't have an on-the-record, dated quote about this specific deal to point to. Separately, the Credit Card Competition Act — reintroduced in Congress in January 2026 — would force banks over $100 billion in assets to route credit transactions through a second, non-Visa/Mastercard network, echoing the debit routing rules that phased in under Durbin during 2011–2012, on the theory that forcing networks to compete will push interchange down the way it partly did for debit. The bill got a real, if narrow, boost that same month when President Trump posted his support on Truth Social, calling on Congress to "stop the out of control Swipe Fee ripoff" — a claim corroborated by trade press, not just the bill sponsors' own release. That's genuine momentum for a bill that's died in committee for years against bank and network lobbying, but it's a personal endorsement, not enacted administration policy, and this particular fight has seen presidential positions shift before.

Other places just capped the fee at the source instead of litigating the symptom. The EU has capped interchange at 0.3% for credit and 0.2% for debit since 2015 — a big reason European cards never developed America's cash-back culture. Australia is going further still: starting October 1, 2026, its central bank is banning surcharging by regulation on the major card networks — eftpos, Mastercard, and Visa — while cutting its own consumer credit interchange cap from 0.8% to 0.3%. American Express sits outside that regulation but is making a parallel, voluntary commitment to drop its own surcharges the same day. Regulatory stick and corporate choice, the practical bet is the same either way: if the fee is small enough at the source, there's nothing left worth fighting about at the register.

Whichever model wins, the underlying lesson holds up better than any single number in this piece. A "free" reward is never free. It's a small charge collected, on average, across a merchant's whole customer base, and redistributed toward whoever qualifies for the shiniest card. What's playing out in U.S. courtrooms and Australian regulatory filings right now is really just a fight over who gets to opt out of paying it — and, underneath that, an argument among economists that hasn't actually been settled about how big "it" is, or in the tightest reading of the data, even which way it runs.

## Where the pop version oversells it

- The opening scene stages a direct, one-to-one transfer between two specific people in line. The real mechanism is a diffuse, store-wide average blended across a merchant's entire customer base over time — vivid shorthand, not a literal transaction.
- The "$149-a-year" figure comes from one 2010 model explicitly labeled "calibrations." A 2013 reexamination found the effect shrinks to a few percent of processing costs under some assumptions and reverses direction entirely under others — the literature disputes the sign, not just the size.
- Interchange isn't a clean three-rung ladder from debit to rewards to premium. It's over a hundred category-specific rates per network, and rewards themselves are funded by a blend of interchange, annual fees, and interest — not by interchange alone.
- The 2023 Fed paper's zip-code income and race findings are real, but its stated mechanism is a sophistication gap that cuts across income levels — subtler than "issuers price low rates at poor people," a framing some reporting on actual rewards-card rates complicates further.

## Open threads

1. The new Visa/Mastercard settlement lets merchants surcharge or decline by card tier for the first time. Once that plays out at real registers, does the cross-subsidy actually shrink — or just relocate?
2. The 2010/2013 dispute was fought entirely with assumption-sensitive models that can't agree on the transfer's sign. Is there a way to estimate it directly from real transaction and pricing data instead?
3. Australia (cap the fee, ban the surcharge) and the U.S. (legalize the surcharge, leave the fee alone) are running opposite bets on the same problem starting the same year. Which actually shrinks the transfer more — and does either one do anything for the unbanked households sitting outside the mechanism entirely?
