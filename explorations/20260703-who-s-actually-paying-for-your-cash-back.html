Who's Actually Paying for Your Cash Back

Two people, back to back at Target. One pays cash. One taps a $795-a-year Sapphire Reserve and banks points. Identical receipt. Somebody's paying for those points — and it isn't Chase, or Target.

Not a literal dollar passing between them. It's a store-wide, time-averaged effect — a fair dramatization of an average. Hold that thought; the rest holds up.
The plumbing

The swipe fee

Every swipe, the merchant's bank pays a fee to the bank that issued the card — the interchange fee. Each network publishes 100+ category rates, and the rule of thumb holds: the richer the rewards, the higher the fee.

Why cash doesn't save you

It's baked into the sticker

For decades, network rules banned charging extra for premium cards. Surcharging is legal now in all but three states — Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maine — yet it still hasn't caught on.

So the average card cost gets folded into the shelf price. Everyone pays $4.99 for the cereal — cash payers included, though they generate none of the cost.

The number everyone quotes

$149 a year — maybe

A 2010 Boston Fed model — its own subtitle is "Theory and Calibrations" — put the transfer at $149/yr, average cash household to card users. And it runs uphill:

The rebuttal

…or maybe backwards

A 2013 reexamination pushed back. Change the assumptions and the effect goes "small, non-existent, or reversed" — in one scenario, card users subsidize cash payers. Two models that can't agree on the direction.

Meanwhile the fees keep climbing — pointing the unresolved number up, not down.

The fight, right now

Two continents, opposite bets

🇺🇸 Legalize the opt-out

Merchants suing since 2005. A revised settlement (Nov 2025, preliminary approval June 2026) would let stores surcharge or decline premium cards by tier. The Credit Card Competition Act (Jan 2026) drew a Trump boost.

🇪🇺 Cap the fee

Interchange capped at 0.3% credit / 0.2% debit since 2015 — a big reason Europe never grew a cash-back culture.

🇦🇺 Both at once

From Oct 1, 2026: ban surcharging on Visa, Mastercard & eftpos, and cut credit interchange 0.8% → 0.3%. Amex joins voluntarily.

The essay's framing — a lens, not a verdict

A "free" reward is never free. It's a small charge collected, on average, across a merchant's whole customer base — then redistributed toward whoever qualifies for the shiniest card.

The courtroom and regulatory fights are really about who gets to opt out of paying it — over an amount economists still can't agree on the size of, or, in the tightest reading, even which way it runs.
